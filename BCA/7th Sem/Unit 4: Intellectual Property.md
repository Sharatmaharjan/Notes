# Unit 4: Intellectual Property (8 Hrs.)

#### 1. Intellectual Property (IP)

- **Definition:** Intellectual Property (IP) refers to creations of the mind, such as inventions; literary and artistic works; designs; and symbols, names, and images used in commerce . It is a type of property that is **non-tangible** and **non-rivalrous** (can be used by many people without depriving the original owner) .
- **Justification for IP Rights:**
    - **Incentive for Innovation:** Provides creators and inventors with a legal mechanism to monetize their work, encouraging further innovation for the public benefit .
    - **Combat Free-Rider Problem:** Prevents others from copying or using the creation without permission, allowing the owner to recover their investment .
    - **Economic Growth:** Fosters an ecosystem for businesses, startups, and industries to grow by protecting their competitive advantage .
- **Nepal's IP Landscape:**
    - **Governing Law:** Primarily governed by the **Patent, Design, and Trademark Act, 2022 (1965)** .
    - **International Commitments:** Nepal is a signatory to the **Paris Convention (1883)** and the **WTO TRIPS Agreement (1995)** .
    - **Limitation:** Nepal is **not a member** of the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) .
    - **Proposed Reform:** The **Industrial Property Bill, 2025** has been registered in Parliament to replace the 1965 Act, introducing modern concepts like Trade Secrets, Geographical Indications, and Layout Designs . The **Copyright Bill, 2024** proposes the creation of "IP Police" for better enforcement .

#### 2. Copyright

- **Definition:** A legal right granted to the creators of "original forms of expression." It protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself .
- **What it Protects:** Literary works (books, articles), musical compositions, artistic works (paintings, photographs), films, sound recordings, and computer software .
- **Key Rights Granted:**
    - Right to reproduce the work.
    - Right to prepare derivative works.
    - Right to distribute copies.
    - Right to publicly perform or display the work .
- **Nepal Context:**
    - **Current Status:** Governed by the Copyright Act, 2059 (2002). Registration is not mandatory for protection but serves as prima facie evidence in court.
    - **Proposed Reform (Copyright Bill, 2024):** A new draft bill aims to introduce **"IP Police"** . Their role would be to conduct raids, investigate, and stop the production and distribution of counterfeit and pirated goods, addressing the current lack of strong enforcement mechanisms .
- **Real-World Example:**
    - A Nepali musician composes a new song. Copyright ensures that a streaming platform cannot use it in a movie or a telecom company cannot use it as a ringback tone without paying royalties to the musician.
- **Advantages:**
    - Protects a wide range of creative works automatically upon creation.
    - Provides economic rights to creators (royalties).
    - Protects "moral rights" (the right of the author to be credited and to object to distortion).
- **Disadvantages:**
    - Does not protect ideas, only their expression.
    - Enforcement of digital piracy (online sharing) remains a major challenge, even with proposed IP Police .

#### 3. Patent

- **Definition:** An exclusive right granted for an invention, which is a product or a process that provides a new way of doing something or offers a new technical solution to a problem .
- **Nepali Legal Definition (Sec 2 of 1965 Act):** "Any useful invention relating to a new method of process or manufacture, operation or transmission of any material or a combination of materials, or that made on the basis of a new theory or formula" .
- **Criteria for Patentability (Novelty, Utility, Inventive Step):**
    - **New Invention:** Must not have been known or used in Nepal or anywhere else before the application .
    - **Useful to General Public:** Must have industrial applicability .
    - **Non-obvious:** Must involve an inventive step that is not obvious to someone skilled in that field .
- **Nepal Context:**
    - **Term:** Protection is granted for **7 years** from the date of registration .
    - **Renewal:** Can be renewed twice, each time for another 7 years (maximum 21 years total) .
    - **Filing:** Applications are filed at the **Department of Industry (DOI)** . Nepal is not a PCT member, so international patents must be filed directly in Nepal within 12 months of the first filing (claiming Paris Convention priority) .
    - **Statistics:** Only **89 patents** have been registered in Nepal as of 2025, indicating low awareness and utilization .
- **Real-World Example:**
    - An inventor in Nepal creates a new, more efficient type of irrigation pump that uses less fuel. By patenting it, they can prevent other companies from manufacturing and selling the exact same pump design for the duration of the patent.
- **Advantages:**
    - Provides a strong, exclusive monopoly over the invention.
    - Allows the patent holder to license the technology for revenue.
- **Disadvantages:**
    - Expensive and time-consuming to obtain.
    - Requires full public disclosure of the invention (which goes into the public domain after the patent expires).

#### 4. Trade Secrets

- **Definition:** Information that has independent economic value because it is not generally known or readily ascertainable, and is subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy .
- **What it Protects:** Formulas (e.g., Coca-Cola recipe), practices, processes, designs, instruments, patterns, or compilations of information (e.g., customer lists, marketing strategies) .
- **Nepal Context:**
    - **Current Law (1965 Act):** Has **no specific provisions** for the protection of trade secrets .
    - **Proposed Reform (Industrial Property Bill, 2025):** Chapter 8, Section 81 of the new bill specifically provides for the protection of commercially valuable confidential information or trade secrets for the first time in Nepal .
- **Real-World Example:**
    - The recipe for "Juju Dhau" (King Yogurt) from Bhaktapur. If the specific process and ingredients are kept as a closely guarded secret by a family or cooperative, it is a trade secret. A former employee cannot legally take that recipe to start a competing yogurt business.
- **Advantages:**
    - No registration is required, and protection can potentially last forever (as long as it remains secret).
    - Protects information that is not patentable (like customer lists).
- **Disadvantages:**
    - If the secret is independently discovered or reverse-engineered (legally), protection is lost.
    - Difficult to enforce once the secret is leaked.

#### 5. Intellectual Property Issues

- **Plagiarism:**
    - **Definition:** The act of presenting someone else's work, ideas, or words as your own without proper attribution. It is an ethical violation, though not always a legal one (e.g., plagiarizing a public domain work may not be illegal but is still unethical).
    - **In Academics/IT:** Copying code from an open-source project without giving credit to the original author, or submitting a purchased term paper.
    - **Prevention:** Using plagiarism detection software, proper citation, and fostering a culture of academic/professional integrity.
- **Reverse Engineering:**
    - **Definition:** The process of taking apart a product, system, or software to understand how it works, often to create a similar product or find interoperability information .
    - **Legal vs. Illegal:**
        - **Legal:** Reverse engineering for interoperability, security research, or learning is often protected (e.g., clean room reverse engineering).
        - **Illegal:** If it is prohibited by a license agreement (like in many End User License Agreements - EULAs) or if it leads to direct copying of patented technology or trade secrets .
    - **Example:** A company legally buys a competitor's software, decompiles it to understand its file format, and then creates a new program that can read/write that format. If they copy the code itself, it becomes infringement.
- **Open Source Code:**
    - **Definition:** Software whose source code is made available for anyone to use, study, modify, and distribute.
    - **Ethical/Legal Issue - License Compliance:** Open Source is **not** the same as public domain. It comes with licenses (e.g., GPL, MIT, Apache). Exploitation occurs when businesses use open-source code without complying with the license terms (e.g., failing to release their derivative source code when required by a "copyleft" license like GPL) .
    - **Example:** A company builds a commercial web application using a library licensed under GPL. The GPL requires them to release their entire application's source code if they distribute it, which they fail to do, violating the license .
- **Competitive Intelligence (CI):**
    - **Definition:** The legal and ethical gathering of information about competitors, the market, and the business environment to aid in strategic decision-making.
    - **Distinction from Industrial Espionage:** CI uses public sources (annual reports, websites, press releases, customer interviews). Industrial espionage uses illegal means (theft, hacking, bribery) to obtain trade secrets .
    - **Example:** A company analyzes a rival's job postings to infer they are moving into a new technology area. This is CI. Paying someone to dumpster-dive at the rival's office for confidential memos is espionage.
- **Trademark Infringement:**
    - **Definition:** The unauthorized use of a trademark or service mark on or in connection with goods and/or services in a manner that is likely to cause confusion, deception, or mistake about the source of the goods/services .
    - **Nepal Context:**
        - **Governing Law:** Patent, Design and Trademark Act, 1965 .
        - **Definition of Trademark:** Any word, symbol, picture, or combination thereof used to distinguish goods or services of one person from another .
        - **Responsible Body:** Department of Industry (DOI) .
        - **Procedure:** File a single-class application with fee NPR 1,000. After examination for conflicts, it is registered (fee NPR 5,000) and published for opposition .
        - **Term:** Valid for 7 years, renewable indefinitely .
        - **Statistics:** ~64,000 trademarks registered in Nepal .
    - **Example:** A local cafe opening named "Starbucks Coffee" and using a similar green mermaid logo would be trademark infringement.
- **Cybersquatting:**
    - **Definition:** The practice of registering, trafficking in, or using a domain name with bad-faith intent to profit from the goodwill of a trademark belonging to someone else .
    - **Common Scenarios:**
        - Registering a famous brand name as a domain (e.g., `nabilbank.org`) and trying to sell it back to the company at an inflated price.
        - Registering a common misspelling of a popular website (typosquatting) to capture traffic.
    - **Nepal Context:** While Nepal does not have a specific law named "Anti-Cybersquatting Act," disputes are handled under:
        - **The Trademark Act (1965):** If the domain name infringes on a registered trademark.
        - **Electronic Transaction Act (ETA), 2008:** As a form of cybercrime or fraud.
        - **ICANN's UDRP (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy):** An international mechanism to resolve disputes for generic top-level domains (.com, .org, .net) .
