#### 1. Privacy Protection and the Law

- **Information Privacy:**
    - **Definition:** The right of individuals to control how their personal information is collected, used, disclosed, and stored by others, including governments and corporations .
    - **Scope:** Covers physical privacy (body, residence), communication privacy (letters, emails, calls), and informational privacy (data, documents, property details) .
- **Constitutional Basis (Nepal):**
    - **Article 28 of the Constitution of Nepal:** Guarantees the "Right to Privacy" as a fundamental right. It protects the privacy of a person's body, residence, property, documents, data, correspondence, and character .
- **Key Privacy Laws in Nepal:**
    - **The Privacy Act, 2075 (2018):** The primary legislation enacted to give effect to the constitutional right to privacy .
    - **Electronic Transaction Act (ETA), 2063 (2008):** Provides legal recognition to electronic records and criminalizes unauthorized access, data theft, and privacy breaches in the digital space .
    - **Muluki Criminal (Code) Act, 2074 (2017):** Contains a separate chapter on privacy offences, such as unauthorized tapping, breach of confidentiality, and trespassing .
    - **Consumer Protection Act, 2075 (2018):** Protects consumer data and mandates transparency and security in digital transactions .

##### 1.1. The Privacy Act, 2075 (2018) - Detailed Provisions 

- **What is "Personal Information"?** The Act provides a specific list, which is narrower than international standards like GDPR.
    - **Identity:** Full name, address, phone number, email address .
    - **Official IDs:** Citizenship number, passport, voter ID, driving license, national ID card details .
    - **Demographics:** Caste, ethnicity, religion, marital status .
    - **Biometrics:** Fingerprints, retina scans, palm lines, blood group .
    - **Other:** Educational qualifications, criminal history, personal letters, opinions expressed by a professional in a decision-making process .
- **What is "Sensitive Information"?** Subject to stricter processing rules .
    - Caste, ethnicity, and origin
    - Political affiliation
    - Religion or faith
    - Physical or mental health conditions
    - Sexual orientation
    - **Property details** (This is a notable feature of Nepali law, unlike in some jurisdictions like Japan) .
- **Obligations for Data Handlers (Public & Private Entities)** :
    - **Consent:** Must obtain explicit, informed consent before collecting personal data.
    - **Purpose Limitation:** Data can only be used for the specific purpose for which it was collected.
    - **Storage & Security:** Must protect and preserve data from unauthorized access or breaches.
    - **Disclosure:** Cannot share personal data with third parties without consent.
    - **Correction:** Individuals have the right to correct inaccurate information held by public entities.
- **Offences and Penalties** :
    - Violation of the Act is a criminal offence.
    - **Penalty:** Imprisonment up to **3 years** OR a fine up to **NPR 30,000** OR both.
    - **Compensation:** The victim is entitled to damages.
    - **Filing a Complaint:** Must be filed at the relevant District Court within **3 months** from the date of the violation.
    - **State vs. Private Case:** Some offences (like warrantless body search, unauthorized drone use) are prosecuted by the state, while others must be filed by the victim as a private case .

##### 1.2. Applications and Court Rulings (Nepal) 

- **Application - Data Collection by Businesses:**
    - E-commerce sites, apps, and online platforms must now have transparent privacy policies.
    - They must explicitly state what data is collected, why, and how it will be used. Unapproved third-party data sharing is prohibited .
- **Application - Media and Public Figures:**
    - Publishing information or photos of a person holding a public office is not an offence if it serves **public interest** or **transparency**.
    - **Challenge:** The Act does not define "public figure," creating ambiguity and potential for litigation .
- **Key Court Rulings:**
    - **Case Reference: N.K.P. 2074, Decision Number: 9740:** The Supreme Court ruled that the government cannot arbitrarily interfere in personal communication (e.g., accessing call details) even in the name of a criminal investigation, without proper legal authority .
    - **Case Reference: N.K.P. 2077, Decision Number: 1232:** The Court ruled that revealing personal information without a legal reason violates the right to privacy and the right to live with dignity, and can lead to discrimination .

##### 1.3. Issues of Concern in Nepali Privacy Law 

- **Overlap with Criminal Code:** The same offence (e.g., unauthorized body search) has different penalties and procedures under the Privacy Act and the Criminal Code, causing legal ambiguity.
- **Narrow Definition of Personal Data:** The Act's list-based definition is restrictive. It does not explicitly cover modern digital identifiers like **IP addresses, cookies, location data, or online behavior**, limiting its effectiveness in the digital age .
- **No "Data Controller" or "Processor" Concept:** The Act does not define these key roles, making it unclear who is liable (the company collecting data or a third-party processing it) in case of a breach .

#### 2. Key Privacy and Anonymity Issues

- **Consumer Profiling:**
    - **Definition:** The process of collecting and analyzing data about consumers' online behavior, purchases, and preferences to create detailed profiles. These are used for targeted advertising, personalized recommendations, and even risk assessment.
    - **Real-World Example:** Amazon or Daraz recommending products based on your browsing and purchase history. Facebook/Instagram showing ads for hiking gear after you searched for "trekking in Nepal."
    - **Ethical Issues:** Lack of transparency (consumers often don't know they are being profiled), potential for manipulation, and use of profiles for price discrimination or denying services.
- **Electronic Discovery (e-Discovery):**
    - **Definition:** The process in legal proceedings where parties are required to produce electronically stored information (ESI) such as emails, documents, social media posts, and databases as evidence.
    - **Real-World Example:** In a company lawsuit, both sides must hand over relevant internal emails and Slack messages. In Nepal, the ETA 2063 recognizes "electronic records" as legally valid evidence .
    - **Privacy Concern:** It can lead to the disclosure of vast amounts of personal or sensitive information not directly related to the case, raising issues of overreach and privacy violation.
- **Workplace Monitoring:**
    - **Definition:** Employers using technology to observe and track employees' activities during work hours. This includes monitoring emails, internet usage, keystrokes, video surveillance, and GPS tracking of company devices.
    - **Real-World Example:** A company in Kathmandu using software to track which websites employees visit and how much time they spend on social media. Installing CCTV in office premises.
    - **Ethical & Legal Issues :**
        - **Privacy Act:** Installing CCTV in private residences without consent is criminalized. In the workplace, the line is blurry.
        - **Balancing Act:** The employer's right to ensure productivity and security vs. the employee's right to privacy. Secret or excessively intrusive monitoring is generally considered unethical.
- **Surveillance:**
    - **Definition:** Close observation of a person or group, especially by a governmental authority. This can range from CCTV in public places to mass interception of digital communications.
    - **Real-World Example:** The Nepal government's directive to ISPs to block certain apps or websites . State monitoring of social media for content deemed harmful to sovereignty or national unity .
    - **Ethical & Legal Issues:**
        - **Constitutional Limits:** Article 28 protects privacy, and Article 17 protects freedom of expression .
        - **Proportionality:** Surveillance must be authorized by law, necessary, and proportionate to a legitimate aim (e.g., national security). Mass, untargeted surveillance is often seen as a violation of fundamental rights .
        - **The Social Media Ban (2025-2026):** The government's attempt to ban 26 platforms for non-registration and the proposed Social Media Bill with broad surveillance powers sparked massive protests and was eventually withdrawn, highlighting the intense public debate over state surveillance vs. freedom .

#### 3. First Amendment Rights & Freedom of Expression: Key Issues

- **First Amendment Rights (US Context):**
    - **Note:** While the US "First Amendment" is specific to the American Constitution, the global concept of freedom of expression is a fundamental human right.
- **Freedom of Expression in Nepal :**
    - **Constitutional Basis:** **Article 17 (2)(a)** of the Constitution of Nepal guarantees every citizen the "Freedom of opinion and expression."
    - **International Law:** This aligns with Nepal's obligations under the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Article 19) and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights .
    - **Not an Absolute Right:** The Constitution allows for "reasonable restrictions" on this freedom. These restrictions can be imposed by law for reasons such as :
        - Sovereignty, territorial integrity, or national independence of Nepal.
        - Harmonious relations between federal units or between various castes, tribes, or communities.
        - Acts of defamation or contempt of court.
        - Incitement to an offence.
        - Acts contrary to public decency or morality.
- **Key Issues Related to Freedom of Expression (with Nepali Context):**
    - **Hate Speech and Misinformation:** The spread of false information and hate speech online is a major global challenge. In Nepal, this is often linked to caste, ethnicity, or religion. The now-withdrawn Social Media Bill attempted to criminalize "false or misleading information" with harsh penalties .
    - **Content Moderation and Censorship:**
        - **The 2025 Social Media Ban:** The government's move to block 26 major platforms for failing to register under the Social Media Directive was widely criticized as an act of censorship that infringed upon citizens' constitutional rights .
        - **Chilling Effect:** Vague laws (e.g., against content harming "national unity") can create a "chilling effect," where people self-censor for fear of prosecution, even if their speech is legitimate .
    - **Balancing with Public Order:** The government has the power to restrict assemblies and expressions to maintain public order. For instance, the Lalitpur District Administration Office was criticized for banning gatherings for six months, exceeding the two-month limit prescribed by the Local Administration Act, thereby infringing on the right to assemble .
    - **Digital Rights and Access:** Banning platforms like TikTok or Facebook is seen by many as denying citizens the primary medium for their expression in the modern age. The 2025 ban was met with widespread protests, especially from Gen Z, demonstrating that restricting access to digital tools is viewed as an attack on free speech itself .

#### 4. Social Networking Ethical Issues

Social networking platforms create unique ethical dilemmas due to their scale, reach, and ability to amplify behavior.

- **Privacy in a Public Sphere:** Users often share personal information publicly or semi-publicly, leading to a false sense of privacy. Ethical issues arise when this data is used by the platform or other users without consent.
    - **Example:** A photo from a private party is shared on Facebook and then used by an employer to make a hiring decision.
- **Cyberbullying and Harassment:** Social media provides a platform for anonymous or semi-anonymous bullying, trolling, and targeted harassment.
    - **Nepali Context:** INSEC reported that in 2023, **88 girls and 131 women** fell victim to crimes via social media, compounded by low technological literacy hindering legal action .
- **Spread of Misinformation and Disinformation:** The rapid, uncritical sharing of information on social media makes it a powerful vector for fake news, which can incite panic, violence, or communal tension.
- **Filter Bubbles and Echo Chambers:** Algorithms that show users content they agree with can trap them in "echo chambers," reinforcing existing beliefs, polarizing society, and making constructive dialogue difficult.
- **Mental Health:** Ethical concerns exist around platform design that encourages addictive behavior, social comparison, and anxiety, particularly among young users.
- **Data Mining and Manipulation:** Platforms analyze user data for targeted advertising, but this capability can be weaponized for political micro-targeting and manipulation, as seen in global elections and referendums.
- **Anonymity vs. Accountability:** While anonymity can protect whistleblowers and activists in oppressive regimes, it can also embolden trolls, harassers, and those spreading hate speech by shielding them from accountability.
- **Digital Divide:** The ethical imperative to ensure equal access and digital literacy is crucial. Without it, a segment of the population is excluded from the benefits of connectivity and is more vulnerable to online harms .
